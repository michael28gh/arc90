# The Zeigarnik Effect Habit Trap

Topic: Efecto Zeigarnik for habit loops

Hook: Your brain hates unfinished habits.

## Slides

1. 1 LOOP - quietly hijacking your habits.
   You don't need more pressure. You need closed loops.
   Learn why unfinished habits keep stealing mental energy.

2. mistake #1: leaving the loop open.
   the problem: You start the habit, miss the follow-through, then carry it in your head all day. Your brain keeps pinging the unfinished task.
   why it matters: The Zeigarnik Effect makes open loops louder than completed ones. That mental noise drains focus and makes restarting harder.
   the fix: Close the loop daily. Track one clear action in Arc90: done or missed. Your brain needs completion evidence.

3. mistake #2: tracking intentions, not completions.
   the problem: Writing “work out more” feels productive, but it does not close anything. The loop stays vague.
   why it matters: Vague goals create infinite open tabs in your mind. Binary tracking gives the brain a clean endpoint.
   the fix: Define the smallest observable rep. Walk 20 minutes. Read 10 pages. Sleep by 11. Then check it off.

4. mistake #3: letting misses become mental debt.
   the problem: One missed habit becomes a story you keep replaying. That story takes more energy than the comeback.
   why it matters: Unfinished loops compound when they stay invisible. A visible reset turns guilt into data.
   the fix: Never miss twice. Log the miss, protect tomorrow, and keep the 90-day protocol alive.

# Goodhart Law Habit Trap

Topic: Ley de Goodhart for habit tracking

Hook: Your habit tracker can lie to you.

## Slides

1. 1 METRIC - quietly lying to your habits.
   You don't need more data. You need the right data.
   Learn why the wrong metric makes habits easier to fake.

2. mistake #1: making the metric the mission.
   the problem: You chase the number instead of the behavior. The tracker starts looking good while the habit stays weak.
   why it matters: Goodhart’s Law says when a measure becomes the target, it stops being a good measure. Bad tracking rewards the wrong game.
   the fix: Track the input, not the ego metric. Arc90 keeps the daily action clear enough to be honest.

3. mistake #2: tracking vanity progress.
   the problem: You count streak length but ignore streak quality. A fragile streak can still look impressive.
   why it matters: A metric should reveal the truth, not protect the story. The goal is behavior change, not a pretty dashboard.
   the fix: Use one binary habit check. Did you do the real action today? Yes or no. Keep the signal clean.

4. mistake #3: gaming your own system.
   the problem: You lower the bar after the day gets hard, then pretend the protocol worked. That is not adaptation. That is avoidance.
   why it matters: When the rule keeps changing, the data becomes useless. You cannot learn from a moving target.
   the fix: Set the minimum dose before the day starts. Run the rule for 90 days before judging it.

# The Moral Licensing Trap

Topic: Moral Licensing Effect for consistency

Hook: One good day can ruin your streak.

## Slides

1. 1 GOOD DAY - quietly ruining your streak.
   You don't need a reward day. You need consistency rules.
   Learn why one good action can give your brain permission to drift.

2. mistake #1: earning permission to quit.
   the problem: You hit the habit once, then act like you bought a day off. One good rep becomes a license to drift.
   why it matters: Moral licensing makes good behavior feel like credit. But habits are built by repetition, not points.
   the fix: Do not spend the win. Log it, keep the chain, and let tomorrow count too.

3. mistake #2: confusing intensity with identity.
   the problem: A huge workout or perfect morning feels like proof you changed. Then the next day drops back to zero.
   why it matters: Identity is shaped by what you repeat, not what you occasionally overdo. Intensity fades. Consistency compounds.
   the fix: Choose the repeatable version. Arc90 is built for daily proof, not heroic bursts.

4. mistake #3: rewarding the wrong behavior.
   the problem: You reward one good day by breaking the system that created it. That trains inconsistency.
   why it matters: Your brain repeats what gets rewarded. If skipping follows success, success becomes the trigger for skipping.
   the fix: Reward the streak, not the escape. Keep the minimum dose alive even on “earned” rest days.
