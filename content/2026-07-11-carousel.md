### SLIDE 2

[01/05]
mistake #1: phone before protocol
⚠ the problem: The first dopamine hit of your day goes to a feed, not your habit. You've spiked your baseline before collecting a single data point.
🎯 why it matters: An elevated dopamine baseline makes low-stimulation habits feel like punishment. Your experiment is contaminated before it begins.
✓ the fix: Phone stays out of reach until habit #1 is logged. Input order is a variable — control it. Track it for 7 days and watch what changes.

### SLIDE 3

[02/05]
mistake #2: running 10 variables at once
⚠ the problem: 10 new habits = 10 uncontrolled variables. When one collapses, they all collapse, and you can't isolate why.
🎯 why it matters: No scientist changes everything at once. You get zero signal on what actually works — just noise and a failed month.
✓ the fix: One habit. One 90-day protocol cycle. Master the variable, then stack the next one.

### SLIDE 4

[03/05]
mistake #3: no tracking
⚠ the problem: You're relying on memory, and memory is a corrupted dataset. You think you were consistent last month. You weren't.
🎯 why it matters: Track it or it didn't happen. No data means no feedback loop, and no feedback loop means no adaptation.
✓ the fix: Log it daily. Binary metric: done / not done. 10 seconds a day buys you 90 days of clean data on yourself.

### SLIDE 5

[04/05]
mistake #4: waiting for motivation
⚠ the problem: Motivation is a noisy signal — it spikes on day 1 and crashes by day 9. You built your system on your least reliable input.
🎯 why it matters: Protocols don't run on mood. The subjects who finish 90 days aren't more motivated. They just don't consult their feelings first.
✓ the fix: Set a fixed trigger — same time, same cue. Run the rep whether you feel it or not. Consistency is the metric, not enthusiasm.

### SLIDE 6

[05/05]
mistake #5: zero accountability
⚠ the problem: Private experiments die quietly. Nobody sees you skip, so skipping costs nothing.
🎯 why it matters: You're the scientist AND the subject — and subjects behave differently when observed. That's not a bug. Use it.
✓ the fix: Make the streak visible. Tell one person about your protocol. Review your data weekly like a lab report.
