### SLIDE 2 — 01/05

**mistake #1: designing for motivation, not friction**

**the problem:** You wait to feel ready instead of making the habit easy to start. Motivation is a variable you can't control.

**why it matters:** Every extra step between you and the habit is a failure point. Twenty seconds of friction is enough to kill a rep.

**the fix:** Cut setup time to near zero. Gym clothes laid out, book on the pillow, bottle already filled. Make the first step stupid-easy.

### SLIDE 3 — 02/05

**mistake #2: keeping the trigger in sight**

**the problem:** Phone on the desk. Snacks on the counter. The cue for the bad habit is always visible.

**why it matters:** You don't decide to check your phone — the cue decides for you. Visible triggers fire automatically.

**the fix:** Add 20 seconds of friction to bad defaults. Phone in another room, snacks out of reach. Out of sight, out of the loop.

### SLIDE 4 — 03/05

**mistake #3: one space, every mode**

**the problem:** You work, scroll, eat and sleep in the same spot. Your brain gets zero context signal.

**why it matters:** Spaces carry associations. If your bed means scrolling, it stops meaning sleep.

**the fix:** Assign one job per zone. Desk = deep work. Bed = sleep. Couch = rest. Let the room run the protocol for you.

### SLIDE 5 — 04/05

**mistake #4: hiding your own data**

**the problem:** Your tracker lives in an app folder you never open. Your progress is invisible.

**why it matters:** What you don't see, you don't repeat. A visible streak is an environmental cue too.

**the fix:** Put the scoreboard in your line of sight. Home screen widget, wall calendar, daily checkmark. Track it or it didn't happen.

### SLIDE 6 — 05/05

**mistake #5: never resetting the lab**

**the problem:** Clutter compounds — dishes, tabs, mess. Tomorrow starts inside yesterday's chaos.

**why it matters:** Every object out of place is a decision waiting to tax you. Entropy compounds, just like streaks do.

**the fix:** Run a 10-minute nightly reset. Close the loops, stage tomorrow's first habit. You're prepping the experiment, not cleaning.
